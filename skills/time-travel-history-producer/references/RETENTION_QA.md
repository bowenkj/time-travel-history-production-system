# Retention and Story QA

## Reference-derived pattern

Strong immersive-history episodes begin inside a practical problem. They translate
history into access, status, cost, danger, bodily sensation, etiquette, objects and
human encounters. The viewer follows a mission rather than waiting through a lecture.

Observed reference behavior from Chloe VS History:

- Titanic establishes place/date, ticket class, modern price and the goal of entering
  first class in roughly the first 16 seconds.
- Tudor London establishes arrival, sensory conflict, immediate itinerary and the
  promise of meeting Henry VIII in roughly the first 25 seconds.
- Stronger episodes contain populated interiors, changing rules and social access;
  scenery alone is insufficient.

Copy the grammar, not the creator's persona or wording.

## Hook gate

By 0:05 show a consequence, problem or anomaly. By 0:15 establish why it matters. By
0:35 clarify the mission without resolving the main curiosity gap.

Reject hooks that:

- require a history lecture to understand the first image;
- show random “exciting” clips without a complete conflict beat;
- end a sentence or human action mid-thought without deliberate suspense;
- summarize the entire event instead of opening a question.

## Story movement

A meaningful state change alters at least one of:

- goal;
- obstacle;
- knowledge;
- choice;
- relationship;
- location;
- proof;
- risk.

A camera cut, new angle or louder music does not count.

For a 10–15 minute episode:

- create a micro change roughly every 8–12 seconds;
- create a material state change roughly every 30–60 seconds;
- build 5–7 tension waves;
- use at least two false victories;
- place a consequence or aftershock after the main physical payoff.

## Tension-wave test

For every sequence, write:

1. setup;
2. conflict or setback;
3. local climax/payoff;
4. new danger created.

If the payoff closes tension without creating a necessary next question, compress the
sequence or move it later.

Vary conflict:

- moral;
- procedural;
- physical;
- social/status;
- identity/performance;
- interpersonal;
- pursuit/time pressure.

Do not invent conflict when the record supplies quieter but real constraints.

## Character agency

- Introduce a person when their skill, choice or vulnerability becomes necessary.
- Do not run consecutive biography cards.
- Give each named protagonist an observable contribution.
- Do not let the recurring host take the decisive action from a real participant.
- During the climax, reduce explanatory narration and allow action, dialogue and sound
  to carry comprehension.

## Information control

- Explain only the rule needed for the next decision.
- Delay secondary motives, documents and consequences until they create a new loop.
- Do not repeat facts already proven by the frame, object or dialogue.
- Treat uncertainty as tension when honest: conflicting accounts, missing permissions,
  unknown outcomes.

## Audio contour

Rollercoaster pacing requires contrast. Use lower intensity or near-silence before a
key choice, failed mechanism, exposed walk or confrontation. Reserve the strongest cue
for the largest earned escalation. Give the music an ending before the brand ident or
chapter transition.

## Scorecard

Score 1–5:

- hook clarity and stakes;
- question-ladder strength;
- false-victory quality;
- conflict variety;
- character agency;
- visual proof density;
- explanation compression;
- climax delivery;
- post-climax retention;
- factual grounding.

Do not pass the storyline with any score below 4 or an overall average below 4.5.
