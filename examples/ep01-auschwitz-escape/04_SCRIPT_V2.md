# EP01 Script V2 — I Escaped Auschwitz With Four Prisoners

Status: **host-led paper script for continuity QA**  
Target runtime: 10:45–11:30  
Format: participant-mode time-travel vlog / historical reconstruction  

## Editorial and disclosure contract

- Bendera, Piechowski, Jaster, Lempart and the core 20 June 1942 escape are historical.
- The host is a fictional fifth participant in an explicitly dramatized time-travel
  scenario. Inside the story, the plan has always included five people.
- The host may work, hide, change, ride and react with the group. The four historical
  men retain their documented contributions and the decisive final-barrier actions.
- Historical dialogue is limited to testimony-supported material. Host dialogue and
  host interaction are dramatization.
- No portal, countdown or travel montage. The modern launch cuts directly into 1942.
- No jokes, challenge-show triumphalism or atrocity spectacle.

# 0:00–0:12 — COLD OPEN: THE BARRIER DOES NOT MOVE

**VISUAL**  
Handheld selfie from the rear of the moving open-top Steyr. The host wears a lower-rank
SS disguise. Bendera drives. Piechowski sits front passenger. Jaster and Lempart are
compressed beside the host. The outer barrier grows larger behind the host's shoulder.

**HOST — low, urgent**

> Okay. This is bad.
>
> There are five of us in a stolen SS car. We are twenty metres from freedom—and the
> guard is not opening the barrier.

The car stops. Engine idle. The host turns the camera toward Piechowski.

**LEMPART — testimony-supported translated line**

> Kazek... do something.

Piechowski opens the door. Cut before he speaks.

**AUDIO**  
Engine, gravel, one tightening pulse. Music drops on the stop. Door latch is the cut.

# 0:12–0:18 — BRAND CROSSING

**VISUAL**  
Six-second ident over five fast evidence beats: damaged lock, rubbish wheel, rank tab,
Steyr key, barrier crank.

**AUDIO**  
The cold-open pulse continues. One metal lock strike lands on the logo and carries
into the modern studio.

# 0:18–0:45 — MODERN MISSION BRIEFING

**VISUAL**  
Host direct to camera in a compact YouTuber/podcast setup. A physical route builds
beside her: PRISONER COMPOUND → SS WAREHOUSE / GARAGE → OUTER CHECKPOINT. Five objects
appear only when named.

**HOST**

> On June 20, 1942, four prisoners escaped Auschwitz without cutting the fence. They
> made the camp's own guards let them out.
>
> I am going back to join their plan. To leave, we need a fake work detail, copied
> keys, stolen uniforms, an SS car—and one final guard to believe every part of it.
>
> The first thing we need is not the car. It is a group that does not exist.
>
> Let's go back to Auschwitz in 1942.

**TRANSITION**  
Hard cut on “1942” to the host walking beside the historical garage lane. No visual
travel effect.

# 0:45–1:24 — THE CAR IS ALREADY INSIDE THE TRAP

**VISUAL**  
Selfie-wide. Host now wears striped prisoner clothing. Engines, footsteps and work
noise continue behind her. She turns to show the Steyr garage, then the inner compound,
then the distant outer checkpoint.

**HOST — walking**

> I have been here for less than ten minutes, and I can already see why stealing a car
> is not the same as escaping.
>
> That garage is outside the prisoners' gate. But it is still inside a much larger
> guarded zone. Even if we reach the car, there is another checkpoint on every road
> out.
>
> From where I am standing, you can see the entire problem. The prisoners are behind
> me. The car is over there. Freedom is beyond a barrier none of us can open ourselves.

Bendera works beneath the Steyr's open hood. The host lowers the camera and works
nearby. Piechowski joins them. No staged dialogue is heard; their plan forms through
looks, the vehicle and a rough route.

**HOST — quieter, to camera**

> This is Eugeniusz Bendera. He is a prisoner mechanic. He knows the garage, he knows
> the cars, and he has already chosen this one.
>
> Piechowski later said Bendera believed he was marked to be killed. He needs out. He
> can get us the car.
>
> But driving to the checkpoint as five prisoners would only deliver us directly to
> the guards. Before we move the car, we have to change what every person along this
> route thinks they are looking at.

The host turns the camera toward the distant barrier.

> He cannot make that guard release it.

---

# 1:24–2:10 — THE FIVE-PERSON TEAM

**VISUAL**  
The host takes the fifth position around a rough table. Camera moves continuously as
each historical contribution appears through action.

**HOST**

> The plan only works because these four men can do four different things.

Piechowski hears an SS instruction and repeats it in German.

> Kazimierz Piechowski speaks German. At the gates, he will have to sound as if giving
> orders is normal.
>
> The rest of us do not need to persuade the guards. We need to look as if Piechowski
> has already persuaded us.

Jaster studies a lock, then conceals a folded report beneath his clothing.

> Stanisław Jaster understands the lock route—and he is carrying something he will not
> explain to me yet.

Lempart counts five positions beside a rubbish trolley.

> Józef Lempart makes the work detail look complete.

Bendera places the car key into the center.

> Bendera gives the whole plan a machine.

The host puts her hand beside the other four.

> And I have one job: stay with them, do exactly what they do, and never give a guard a
> reason to count twice.
>
> Five people is not a crowd here. It is a pattern. If one of us moves differently,
> the pattern breaks.

The host looks toward the closed SS repository.

> The uniforms are in there. We are not allowed inside.

# 2:10–3:00 — BREAK THE LOCKS TO CREATE THE KEYS

**VISUAL**  
Selfie close-up near the preparation area. The host watches the corridor while Jaster
damages a lock mechanism. An SS supervisor later finds the fault and brings Bendera to
repair it.

**HOST — whisper**

> We cannot steal the keys. So Jaster is creating a reason for the SS to bring the
> locks to Bendera.
>
> That sounds backwards until you see it happen. The safest way to touch a protected
> lock is to make its owner ask you to repair it.

Cut between Jaster creating the fault, Bendera performing the authorized repair and an
improvised key taking shape.

**HOST — with replica mechanism in a brief direct-to-camera insert**

> Jaster creates the problem. The SS chooses Bendera to fix it. And the repair gives
> him access no prisoner is supposed to have.

Piechowski prepares the coal hatch and rank tabs. Three lock turns land cleanly.

**HOST**

> We now have a quiet way through most of the building.
>
> No chase. No fence cutters. Just a series of doors that should open before anyone
> notices the wrong people are using them.

A fourth lock sound stops halfway.

> Most.

# 3:00–3:45 — SATURDAY: BECOME ORDINARY

**VISUAL**  
Saturday work groups disperse. Host walks inside the moving crowd, filming herself at
arm's length without stopping traffic.

**HOST**

> It is Saturday. Work ends early, the offices around the warehouse will be thinly
> staffed, and this is the only moment when five prisoners moving together might look
> ordinary.

Inside the attic of an unfinished block, the five review the route. The four men pray.
The host lowers the camera and joins the silent moment.

Behind the kitchen, the group fills the trolley with ash and rubbish. The host pushes
from one side. The damaged wheel squeals.

**HOST — moving, breath controlled**

> Our first disguise is not an SS uniform.

She turns the camera to the dirty trolley and the five striped figures.

> It is boredom. We have to look like a job nobody wants to watch.
>
> The problem is that I can hear this wheel from halfway across the yard, and every
> step toward the gate makes it feel louder.

The inner guard looks up from his ledger.

---

# 3:45–4:30 — THE FIRST GATE RECORDS THE LIE

**VISUAL**  
Host selfie whisper from within the five-person detail. Camera drops to chest height as
they reach the guard. No music.

**HOST — barely audible**

> If he checks whether this group exists, this ends here.
>
> Piechowski is about to place a group that exists only in our plan into an official
> ledger. Once that pencil moves, the lie becomes part of the camp's routine.

Piechowski gives his prisoner number and indicates four others. Pencil touches paper.
The host stops breathing. The gate opens.

The trolley fully crosses. The host walks several steps, turns back toward the inner
compound and lets out one small breath.

**HOST**

> It worked.

She rotates the camera forward. The warehouse, towers and distant outer barrier fill
the frame.

> No. It worked once.
>
> We are outside the prisoners' compound. We are still inside the SS guard chain.
>
> The first gate has not released us. It has recorded us moving toward a building we
> have no permission to enter.

The empty repository waits ahead.

---

# 4:30–5:28 — THE KEYS STOP WORKING

**VISUAL**  
The host moves with the group past closed office windows, checks behind them, then
climbs through the prepared coal hatch. Handheld light catches dust and breathing.

**HOST — whisper**

> The building is closed. If anyone sees five prisoners entering it, there is no story
> we can tell.
>
> The offices being empty helps us until we make a sound. Then the same silence carries
> that sound through the entire building.

First internal door: key turns. Second: opens. Third: opens. The group reaches the
weapons-room door. Bendera tries the key. Nothing.

The host watches him try again.

**HOST**

> This is the one room we cannot leave without.
>
> The key worked exactly where Bendera expected—until the door holding the uniforms
> and weapons. We can retreat and lose the plan, or force it and leave proof behind.

Bendera takes the crowbar. Music disappears.

**HOST — to camera, almost silent**

> The quiet plan is over.

Metal strains. The frame cracks loudly. Everyone freezes. Hold long enough to hear the
building after the impact. No footsteps answer.

The door opens onto uniforms and weapons.

---

# 5:28–6:18 — FOUR OFFICERS, ONE PRISONER

**VISUAL**  
Piechowski, Jaster, Lempart and the host change into stolen SS clothing. Avoid glamour
or extended insignia montage. Focus on hands, belts, boots and the danger of being
recognized. Bendera keeps his striped blouse over SS underclothes.

**HOST — walking camera**

> Four of us now look like SS personnel. Bendera still looks like a prisoner.
>
> It feels as if he should change first. The opposite is safer because his real
> prisoner identity is the only identity the garage guards already recognize.

She turns the camera to the garage across the open yard.

> That is deliberate. Guards know the prisoner mechanic moves vehicles. If an unknown
> SS officer opens that garage, they may look twice. If Bendera walks over as himself,
> they may not look at all.

Bendera checks the route. The host adjusts her collar, then looks directly into camera.

> He has to cross alone. We cannot help him without destroying the disguise.
>
> So the four of us who appear armed and powerful have to hide, while the one man still
> dressed as a prisoner walks into the open.

---

# 6:18–7:15 — STEAL THE CAR IN PLAIN SIGHT

**VISUAL**  
The host waits with Piechowski, Jaster and Lempart inside the side entrance. She films
over her shoulder as Bendera crosses the exposed yard. A guard glances toward him.
Everyone goes still.

The guard returns to his work. Bendera reaches the garage and opens it.

**HOST — whisper, eyes on the yard**

> He has driven cars on orders before. Right now, stealing this one looks exactly like
> work.
>
> A guard has just looked straight at him. Bendera does not speed up, slow down or look
> back at us. That may be the most important thing he does on this walk.

Ignition turns. Hesitates. Starts. The Steyr emerges openly.

The host turns the camera toward herself, a restrained flash of relief.

> That is the Steyr 220 used by HWL chief Paul Kreutzmann. It was not the commandant's
> car, despite the version that gets repeated online.

Bendera returns. He finishes changing. Five positions are filled: Bendera driving,
Piechowski front passenger, Jaster and Lempart in the rear, host compressed beside them
with the handheld camera low.

Main clock: approximately 3:45 p.m.

**HOST**

> We have the uniforms. We have the car. We have no pass for the final checkpoint.
>
> And there is no room in here to hide a nervous movement. The guard at the barrier
> will be able to see all five of us.

# 7:15–7:55 — THE DISGUISE WORKS TOO WELL

**VISUAL**  
Handheld from the host's seat. SS personnel salute the Steyr as it passes. Piechowski
returns the acknowledgment without overacting. No one inside celebrates.

**HOST — low, to camera**

> They are saluting us.
>
> The strangest part is having to treat that as normal. If any of us looks surprised,
> the disguise explains itself.

Another salute. The host almost smiles, then sees the barrier ahead.

> Every salute proves the disguise works. It also brings us closer to the one guard who
> can ask for papers we do not have.

Jaster protects the concealed report. Lempart watches the guard booth. Bendera keeps a
steady speed. Barrier remains down.

**HOST**

> That should be moving.

Rejoin the cold-open angle.

---

# 7:55–9:12 — “KAZEK, DO SOMETHING”

**VISUAL / AUDIO**  
No explanatory narration once the car begins its final approach. Use engine, gravel,
breathing and the barrier mechanism.

The host's camera alternates naturally between her face, the guard and the men inside
the car. Distance collapses. Bendera slows. The car stops.

**LEMPART — testimony-supported translated line**

> Kazek... do something.

Piechowski freezes for one beat, opens the door and moves his hand toward his belt and
holster.

**PIECHOWSKI — translated reconstruction; exact German wording varies by account**

> Open the barrier! Now!

The guard runs to the crank. Barrier begins moving. Piechowski remains in role. The
guard salutes. Bendera drives.

Do not cut away before the full rear of the Steyr clears the barrier.

Only then does the host turn the camera back toward herself.

**HOST — breathless, controlled**

> We are out.

She looks behind toward the camp.

> And now they start counting.

# 9:12–10:06 — FREEDOM CREATES A SEARCH

**VISUAL**  
Moving selfie from the rear seat as the Steyr leaves the main road for forest routes.
Intercut the five-person escape with five empty roll-call positions inside the
dramatized scenario, a duty ledger, emergency search personnel and an escape telegram.

**HOST**

> The checkpoint was only one guard. Roll call connects every missing name.
>
> Inside the camp, an emergency SS search unit is being sent out. A telegram is already
> carrying our identities and the description of this car to police and border posts.
>
> There does not need to be a car chasing us for the search to become dangerous. Every
> checkpoint that receives that description can now be waiting before we arrive.

The Steyr loses speed and becomes unusable. Do not show an invented mechanical cause.
The five leave it on a forest route.

**HOST — beside the abandoned car**

> This car defeated the checkpoint. From this moment, it makes us easier to find.
>
> Leaving it feels wrong because it is the reason we are outside. Keeping it would turn
> every road into a test we eventually lose.

The group covers or discards the obvious uniform layers and continues on foot.

Jaster still protects the folded paper beneath his coat.

**HOST**

> He has carried that through every gate. I still do not know what it is.

# 10:06–10:52 — THE SECOND MISSION

**VISUAL**  
The host walks with the group through the forest. Paths begin to split. Jaster finally
reveals the folded report.

**HOST — direct to camera, then turning toward Jaster**

> This escape was carrying something besides us.
>
> Jaster has a report from Witold Pilecki's resistance network inside Auschwitz. It is
> intended for Home Army headquarters. The Museum says he probably completes that
> mission.
>
> Until now, I thought he was protecting something that might save the five of us. He
> was protecting something meant to leave us and keep moving.

Visualize the report moving toward Warsaw while the SS escape telegram spreads in the
opposite direction.

**HOST**

> The same plan that carried five people beyond the guard chain is now carrying
> evidence of what was happening inside it.

The host looks toward the four men as they prepare to separate.

---

# 10:52–11:30 — RETURN AND DEBRIEF

**VISUAL**  
Hard visual return to the modern set. No portal. The host remains in silence for one
beat. Five mechanism objects sit before her: work-detail tally, improvised key, uniform
silhouette, car key, raised barrier model.

Four historical paths animate around the host as one continuous movement:

- Lempart hides near his family's region and survives the war;
- Bendera survives using false papers and mechanical work;
- Piechowski joins the Home Army and survives;
- Jaster enters resistance special operations and dies in unresolved circumstances in
  1943; his parents are imprisoned at Auschwitz and both die.

**HOST**

> I thought this escape was about stealing a car.
>
> It was really about controlling what every guard believed he was seeing: a work
> detail, a mechanic, four officers, an authorized vehicle.
>
> At the final barrier, the plan ran out. What carried it through was Bendera holding
> the car steady, Lempart forcing the moment, and Piechowski becoming the officer the
> guard expected to obey.
>
> Four prisoners created the plan. For a few hours, I got to feel how little room it
> left for fear.

End on the four names and prisoner numbers. No generic motivational moral.

# Performance notes

## Host delivery

- Modern briefing: clear, fast and confident.
- Historical selfie mode: quieter, reactive and physically aware; sentences shorten
  under pressure.
- Do not perform constant panic. Let silence, looking and controlled breathing carry
  exposure.
- The host speaks to the viewer as events happen, not as an omniscient narrator who
  already knows every outcome.

## Historical dialogue

- Keep “Kazek... do something” as the primary testimony-supported line.
- Treat Piechowski's gate order as translated reconstruction and avoid pretending one
  exact English or German version is definitive.
- Do not give the four men invented emotional speeches, arguments or confessions.

## Camera grammar

- Host selfie or host POV anchors every major transition.
- Use populated moving environments behind the host.
- Do not allow three consecutive detached explanatory shots.
- During the stopped-car climax, preserve the complete interaction and reduce host
  speech to zero until the car has physically cleared the barrier.

## Audio contour

- Opening and brand: narrowing mechanical pulse.
- Arrival/team: restrained movement rhythm, no heroic score.
- Locks: tactile metal becomes rhythm.
- Inner gate: near-silence around pencil and ledger.
- Weapons-room failure: remove music; emphasize strain and crack.
- Garage walk: low pulse with exposed gaps.
- Salutes: short false-release lift with unresolved bass.
- Final barrier: engine and breathing dominate; score returns only after crossing.
- Search: pursuit pulse, then thinner forest texture.
- Report/debrief: restrained emotional resolve, not triumph.
