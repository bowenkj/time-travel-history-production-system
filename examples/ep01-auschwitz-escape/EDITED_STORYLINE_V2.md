# EP01 Edited Storyline V2 — I Escaped Auschwitz With Four Prisoners

Status: **channel-format review gate; do not script or storyboard until approved**  
Target runtime: 10:45–11:30  
Primary audience question: **Can I escape Auschwitz with four prisoners by making the
camp's own guards believe we belong there?**

## V2 correction

V1 treated the host as an occasional explainer around a conventional historical
reenactment. V2 restores the channel promise: the host travels into the reconstructed
world and joins Bendera, Piechowski, Jaster and Lempart as the fifth member of the
escape plan from the beginning.

Inside the story, the plan has always included five people. The fake work detail,
uniform supply, car seating and route already accommodate the host; the episode does
not manufacture a separate subplot about an “extra” person. The pressure comes from
the documented escape mechanism—access, locks, disguise, the car and the final
barrier—not from explaining the premise.

The four historical participants and the core escape method are real. The host's role
as the fifth escapee is an explicitly dramatized time-travel scenario. Bendera,
Piechowski, Jaster and Lempart still own their historically documented contributions.

## Channel-format contract

- The host or host POV is the visual anchor in every major transition.
- Historical context answers a problem the host has just encountered.
- The environment stays populated; guards, prisoners and staff create moving social
  pressure rather than serving as background decoration.
- Use selfie-wide, walking direct-to-camera, over-shoulder POV, object interaction and
  immediate reaction. Do not default to detached master shots.
- Outside the uninterrupted final-barrier climax, do not spend more than roughly 20
  seconds without returning to the host, host POV or a host-created question.
- The host works, hides, changes clothing, rides in the car and faces the guards with
  the group, while the four historical men retain their signature actions.
- No playful challenge language, costume gag or suffering-as-spectacle. Immediacy and
  personality come from fear, uncertainty, physical exposure and discovery.
- No portal or travel montage. The modern briefing ends with a spoken launch and cuts
  directly to the host already inside the reconstructed environment.

## One five-person journey, two responsibilities

### The host's responsibility

The host must personally survive five permissions with the group:

1. leave the prisoner compound as a plausible work detail;
2. enter a closed SS repository;
3. become visually authorized;
4. obtain a staff car without triggering suspicion;
5. make the final guard choose obedience over inspection.

At each layer the host discovers that the previous success did not equal freedom.

### The historical four's responsibility

The four prisoners supply the real solution:

- Bendera: plan, keys, garage access and driving;
- Piechowski: German, work-detail report, senior disguise and final bluff;
- Jaster: manufactured lock access and Pilecki's hidden report;
- Lempart: work-detail cover and the prompt that breaks Piechowski's freeze.

The host creates presence and audience identification. The historical four still own
the plan's real engineering and payoff.

## Beat map

### 1. 0:00–0:12 — Cold open: twenty metres from freedom

**Host experience:** Handheld selfie from inside the stopped open-top Steyr. The host
keeps her voice low, turns the camera toward the four men and then toward the guard.

> “There are five of us in a stolen SS car. We are twenty metres from freedom—and the
> guard is not opening the barrier.”

The car stops. The host looks back at the camera as Lempart's documented prompt is
heard from inside: “Kazek... do something.” Piechowski opens the door. Cut before the
order.

**Question created:** How did we reach the final guard, and what can Piechowski say
without papers?

### 2. 0:12–0:18 — Brand crossing

Six seconds maximum. Preserve the engine pulse and barrier tension. Logo/evidence
montage ends on a lock strike that carries into the modern briefing.

### 3. 0:18–0:45 — Modern mission briefing

Podcast/YouTuber direct-to-camera. A simple physical route appears beside the host:
prisoner compound → HWL warehouse and garage → outer checkpoint.

> “On June 20, 1942, four prisoners escaped Auschwitz without cutting the fence. They
> made the camp's own guards let them out. I am going back to join their plan and find
> out whether I can make it through every gate with them.”

Only the five permissions are named: work detail, keys, uniforms, car, final bluff.
No general camp-history lecture.

**Launch:** “The first thing we need is not a car. It is a work group that does not
exist. Let's go back to Auschwitz in 1942.”

Hard cut on “1942” to the host already walking beside the garage lane. No travel
effect.

### 4. 0:45–1:24 — Arrival: the car is already inside the trap

**Host experience:** Selfie-wide in prisoner clothing inside the support area. Engines
and work noise continue behind her. She turns the camera from the Steyr garage to the
inner compound and then to the distant outer checkpoint.

The host initially treats access to the car as the solution. A moving sight-line
reveals why it is not: the car is still trapped between two controlled boundaries.

Cut to Bendera repairing the Steyr. The host works nearby, joins him and Piechowski at
the first planning exchange, and introduces him through what he can do, not biography.

**Historical action:** According to Piechowski's later recollection, Bendera believed
he was marked to be killed and brought the beginning of an escape plan.

**Host realization:** “He could get a car. He could not make a guard release it.”

**Exit question:** What identity could make every guard see routine instead of escape?

### 5. 1:24–2:10 — Meet the five-person escape team

**Host experience:** The host takes the fifth position at the worktable. As each
historical contribution becomes necessary, the relevant person enters the action
rather than appearing on a biography card.

- Piechowski hears and accurately repeats a German instruction.
- Jaster studies the lock route and later conceals a folded report.
- Lempart counts five places beside the rubbish trolley.
- Bendera places the vehicle key into the operational chain.

The host turns the camera between people and objects, allowing the scene to feel
discovered rather than diagrammed.

**Payoff:** A fake rubbish Kommando solves both a paperwork problem and the fear that
a real work group could be punished for losing them.

**Exit question:** The uniforms are inside a building none of them can legally enter.

### 6. 2:10–3:00 — To obtain keys, first create a repair

**Host experience:** Tight selfie beside the real preparation area. The host keeps
watch while Jaster and Bendera create the repair opportunity, then tests an explanatory
replica lock in a quick direct-to-camera insert.

Turn to documented action: Jaster damages three locks; an SS supervisor brings
Bendera to repair them; Bendera uses authorized contact with the mechanisms to create
improvised keys. Piechowski prepares the coal hatch and rank insignia.

The host traces the causal chain with three tactile beats:

> “Jaster creates the fault. The SS chooses Bendera to repair it. The repair gives him
> access the prisoners were never supposed to have.”

Three locks turn. A fourth sound stops halfway.

**Exit question:** The quiet plan works—until the one room containing the weapons.

### 7. 3:00–3:45 — Saturday: become ordinary

**Host experience:** The host enters a crowded transition of work groups ending their
shift. She has to move with the traffic rather than stand and lecture. Sound, bodies
and uniforms create orientation.

The host joins the four men in the attic as they review the route and pray. She then
helps fill and push the rubbish trolley. A brief host POV from behind the cart makes
its noise and conspicuousness physical.

**Historical action:** The five take the positions of a routine rubbish detail.

**Host realization:** “Their first disguise is not an SS uniform. It is boredom.”

**Exit question:** Will the gate ledger expose a work group that does not exist?

### 8. 3:45–4:30 — The first gate records the lie

**Host experience:** Selfie whisper from the side of the gate lane, then immediate
host POV toward the guard and ledger. The host stops talking when the guard looks up.

Piechowski reports his number and four others. The host crosses inside the detail,
turns the camera back toward the inner compound and begins to register relief. Then
she rotates forward: towers, guard line and the distant final barrier remain.

**False victory:** The fake detail worked.

**New danger:** They have not escaped; they have entered a wider guarded zone with no
legitimate reason to enter the now-closed repository.

### 9. 4:30–5:28 — The keys stop working

**Host experience:** The host moves with the group along the exterior route, checks
the empty office windows, and slips with them into the dark coal-entry environment.
Her breathing and coal dust replace formal exposition.

**Historical action:** Prepared hatch opens. Improvised keys carry all five through
the internal route. At the weapons-room door, the key refuses to turn.

The host frames the damaged door and whispers the immediate consequence: forcing it
will leave proof even if the escape succeeds.

Crowbar enters. Music drops. Metal strains. The frame breaks.

**Exit question:** The uniforms solve one identity problem but create another for the
only person who can retrieve the car.

### 10. 5:28–6:18 — Four officers and one visible prisoner

**Host experience:** Walking explanation among changing silhouettes, focused on
identity mechanics rather than a costume montage. The host points out the visual
contradiction before narration resolves it.

**Historical action:** Piechowski, Jaster, Lempart and the host change first. Bendera
keeps the striped prisoner blouse visible over SS underclothes.

**Host question:** “Why would the driver be the only man still dressed as a prisoner?”

The camera turns from Bendera to the garage across the exposed yard. The answer is
spatial and social: guards recognize the prisoner mechanic moving vehicles; an
unknown SS officer opening that garage could attract scrutiny.

**Exit question:** Bendera must cross alone while looking exactly like the person who
is supposed to be trapped inside.

### 11. 6:18–7:15 — Steal the car in plain sight

**Host experience:** The host waits with Piechowski, Jaster and Lempart, alternating
close selfie reactions with over-shoulder views of Bendera crossing the guard sight
lines. She goes silent whenever attention shifts toward him.

**Historical action:** Bendera reaches the garage, opens it with a key and drives out
the Steyr because the action resembles work he has performed before. He returns,
finishes changing and all five occupy their fixed seats.

The host identifies the vehicle in one sentence as the staff car used by HWL chief
Paul Kreutzmann, not the commandant's car.

**False victory:** The engine starts and all five escapees occupy the same vehicle.

**Exit question:** A stolen car is useful only while everyone mistakes it for an
authorized one.

### 12. 7:15–7:55 — The disguise works too well

**Host experience:** Handheld footage from the host's seat inside the car captures SS
personnel saluting as they pass. Her reaction gives the viewer a brief release without
declaring success.

Inside the car, no one celebrates. Jaster protects the hidden report. Lempart watches
the booth. Piechowski maintains rank.

**Host realization:** “Every salute proves the disguise works. It also brings them
closer to the one guard who can ask for papers they do not have.”

The host turns the camera forward to the checkpoint established in the cold open. The
barrier remains down.

### 13. 7:55–9:12 — “Kazek, do something”

The host remains inside the car but stops explaining once it halts. Her camera records
the complete conflict exchange and yields the decisive action to the four men.

**Historical action:** Distance collapses. Bendera slows and stops. Piechowski freezes.
Lempart prompts him. Piechowski opens the door, performs rank, moves his hand toward
the holster and orders the guard to open the barrier. The guard obeys and salutes.
Bendera drives the entire car across before the edit releases tension.

**Host reaction:** Only after the rear clears does the host look back into camera. No
cheering. She turns toward the camp as the alarm problem becomes visible.

**Exit question:** Freedom has created five missing names and one unmistakable stolen
car.

### 14. 9:12–10:06 — The camp begins looking

**Host experience:** Moving selfie from the forest route. The host can hear the Steyr
but also introduces the response forming behind it. Her narration rides moving
footage rather than a static evidence card.

Crosscut the host inside the escaping car with five empty roll-call positions, a duty
ledger, search personnel and an escape telegram carrying identities and vehicle
description.

The Steyr becomes unusable without inventing an exact cause. The host abandons it with
the four men and continues on foot.

**Host realization:** “The car defeated the checkpoint. From this moment, it makes
them easier to identify.”

**Exit question:** Why is Jaster still protecting something beneath his coat after the
physical escape is over?

### 15. 10:06–10:52 — A second mission leaves the camp

**Host experience:** The host walks with the group until the forest paths begin to
split. Only now does Jaster reveal the folded report from Witold Pilecki intended for
Home Army headquarters.

Use a moving courier route, not a static document lecture. The host turns the camera
from the report's route to the camp's telegram spreading in the opposite direction.

**Payoff:** The plan carried five people and evidence beyond the guard chain.

### 16. 10:52–11:30 — Fates and debrief

The host returns from the dramatized journey while the four historical paths resolve
around her rather than stopping for four biography cards:

- Lempart hides near his family's region and survives;
- Bendera survives through false papers and mechanical work;
- Piechowski joins the Home Army and survives;
- Jaster enters resistance special operations and dies in unresolved circumstances;
  his parents are imprisoned at Auschwitz and both die.

Return to direct-to-camera with the five permission objects: work-detail tally, key,
uniform silhouette, car and raised barrier.

> “I thought this escape was about stealing a car. It was really about controlling
> what each guard believed he was seeing—until the last guard stopped believing it.
> At that point, the plan survived because every person in that car knew exactly what
> had to happen next.”

End on the four names, not the host.
