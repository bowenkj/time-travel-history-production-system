# EP01 Raw Story — The Four-Man SS-Car Escape from Auschwitz

Research pass: 2026-07-21
Event date: 20 June 1942
Purpose: establish the factual chronology before writing the edited storyline or
dialogue.

## Factual core

On Saturday 20 June 1942, four Polish prisoners—Eugeniusz Bendera, Kazimierz
Piechowski, Stanisław Gustaw Jaster and Józef Lempart—escaped from the German Nazi
Auschwitz concentration camp. They created a fake four-man rubbish detail, reached
the SS garrison equipment repository (HWL), entered through a prepared coal hatch,
used copied or improvised keys to move through the building, forced the weapons-room
door, put on SS uniforms, took weapons, stole a Steyr 220 staff car and passed the
outer checkpoint by impersonating SS personnel.

This was not one man's improvisation. It depended on four people occupying different
positions inside the camp and combining access, mechanical knowledge, German,
resistance contacts and performance at the final barrier.

## Necessary world context

- Auschwitz was established in 1940 initially for Polish political prisoners and was
  also functioning as a center for the extermination of Jews in 1942.
- The four men were imprisoned in Auschwitz I, the main camp. The HWL and SS garage
  sat outside the inner prisoner compound but inside a wider guarded zone.
- During working hours, a “large guard chain” surrounded the main camp and its support
  infrastructure. Checkpoints and barriers controlled movement across its boundary.
- Passing the inner camp gate as an authorized work detail did **not** equal freedom.
  They still had to cross the outer checkpoint.
- In Piechowski's later account, prisoners were warned that an escape could bring
  lethal retaliation against members of the escapee's real work group or block. He
  said this was why they formed a fake four-man detail rather than disappearing from
  a real Kommando.

Only the first two bullets belong in initial exposition. The security geometry should
be explained when the men clear the first gate and the viewer might mistakenly think
they have escaped.

## Raw chronology

### Before the escape

1. **Piechowski and the camp resistance**
   Piechowski had been in Auschwitz since 20 June 1940. He spoke German and had access
   to SS storage work. Jaster belonged to the camp resistance organization founded by
   Witold Pilecki.

2. **Bendera brings urgency**
   In a 2011 interview, Piechowski said Bendera learned through prisoners with access
   to records that he was marked to be killed. Bendera told him he could obtain a car,
   but a car alone could not pass the camp's layers of security. This motive is
   survivor recollection, not independently shown in the Museum's event chronology;
   attribute it to Piechowski rather than presenting an unseen document.

3. **The plan becomes a four-man fake work detail**
   Piechowski and Bendera recruited Jaster and Lempart. Piechowski later said the fake
   detail was meant to prevent the SS from treating an existing work group as
   responsible for their disappearance.

4. **They manufacture access instead of stealing it outright**
   According to Bendera's archived testimony, he instructed Jaster to damage three
   locks used around their commander's rooms and the internal route. Bendera was then
   assigned to repair them and used the opportunity to make bump keys.

5. **They prepare the building route**
   A hatch leading into the coal storage was opened or disabled in advance. One
   account says Piechowski removed a bolt that morning so the hatch could be reopened.

6. **They prepare the visual disguise**
   Lempart's testimony says Piechowski, who knew sewing, attached rank insignia that
   had been prepared in consultation with camp tailors. The uniforms and weapons
   themselves would be taken from the HWL only after the escape had begun.

7. **Bendera prepares the car**
   Bendera selected and thoroughly repaired an Austrian Steyr 220. His archived
   testimony explicitly says it was used by HWL chief SS-Hauptsturmführer Paul
   Kreutzmann—not Rudolf Höss, the camp commandant. Popular retellings frequently get
   the ownership wrong.

### Saturday, 20 June 1942

8. **They choose the low-staff window**
   It was Saturday. Work normally ended around midday, leaving HWL offices and the
   garage comparatively empty. Piechowski later recalled that the four met in the
   attic of an unfinished block and went over the plan one last time. His account says
   they prayed for their families and agreed not to be taken alive if the plan failed.

9. **They become a rubbish detail**
   After lunch, they left the attic, went behind the camp kitchen, filled a trolley
   with ash and rubbish and presented themselves as a four-person detail taking waste
   outside. Piechowski reported his prisoner number and “three,” indicating the three
   men under his supervision. The guard recorded the movement without a detailed
   check.

10. **They clear the inner gate but remain inside the large guard chain**
    The four moved beyond the main prisoner compound toward the HWL. This is the first
    apparent success and the first opportunity to explain that the outer barrier still
    stood between them and freedom.

11. **They enter the closed HWL building**
    They reached the already-closed repository and slipped through the previously
    prepared hatch into the basement/coal area.

12. **The copied keys mostly work**
    Bendera's bump keys opened the planned internal route. The weapons-storage door
    did not yield to the same method, so they forced it with a crowbar. This is an
    important escalation: the quiet access plan becomes noisy and physically visible.

13. **Three men transform first**
    Piechowski, Jaster and Lempart put on SS clothing and took weapons. Piechowski wore
    the senior-looking uniform. Bendera initially kept his prisoner blouse over SS
    underwear so he could approach the garage in a role guards had seen him perform.

14. **Bendera obtains the car alone**
    Bendera walked from the repository toward the nearby garage, opened it with a key
    and drove out the Steyr 220. Lempart's testimony says this did not itself look
    suspicious because Bendera had previously driven vehicles on Kreutzmann's orders.
    He returned to the side entrance, completed his uniform and the men loaded their
    stolen items.

15. **The four depart at approximately 3:45 p.m.**
    Lempart recalled seeing 3:45 on the main building clock. Wearing helmets, belts and
    rifles, they drove past SS personnel who treated them as officers. This produces a
    dangerous false feeling that the disguise has already worked.

16. **The final barrier remains closed**
    As the Steyr approached the checkpoint at the edge of the large guard chain, the
    guard failed to raise the barrier. Piechowski later remembered the remaining
    distance shrinking—from roughly 80 meters to 20—while Bendera became visibly
    nervous. The car stopped.

17. **“Kazek, do something”**
    In the Museum's published excerpt, Piechowski recalled a monk seated behind him
    striking or prompting him and saying, “Kazek, do something.” This identifies the
    prompt as Lempart. Piechowski opened the door, shouted military abuse in German,
    moved his hand toward his holster and ordered the guard to open the gate. The guard
    obeyed and saluted as the car passed.

18. **They are outside, but pursuit remains possible**
    The men avoided main roads. Accounts agree that the car eventually could take them
    no farther and they continued on foot. The Museum lesson places the breakdown near
    Maków Podhalański; Piechowski's Guardian interview describes driving forest roads
    for about two hours and abandoning it near Wadowice. Do not assert a precise point
    until this discrepancy is resolved.

19. **The camp discovers four missing prisoners**
    The Auschwitz Memorial reproduces the duty officer's note that four men were
    missing at roll call. An emergency search unit drawn from the 1st and 2nd SS guard
    companies was sent out, but the search failed. An escape telegram carrying the
    men's identities was distributed to police and border authorities. This gives the
    post-gate sequence a documented pursuit clock without inventing a cinematic car
    chase.

### After the escape

20. **The group separates**
    Piechowski recalled that Lempart became ill and was left with a parish priest, and
    that Jaster returned toward Warsaw. The Museum chronology says Lempart hid near
    Maków Podhalański in Zachełmna, the area his parents came from, and survived the
    war.

21. **Jaster carries evidence out**
    The Auschwitz Memorial states that Jaster carried a report prepared by Witold
    Pilecki for Home Army headquarters and probably completed the mission. Jaster later
    served in the Home Army special-operations unit Kosa-Osa. He died in Warsaw in
    1943 in circumstances the Museum describes as unresolved. Avoid repeating a
    definitive accusation that he collaborated with the Germans.

22. **Piechowski and Bendera survive**
    The Museum says both reached the Kielce region. Piechowski joined the Home Army and
    fought until the end of the war. Bendera obtained false papers and worked as a
    mechanic for Organisation Todt in Ukraine until the Red Army arrived, then
    returned to Poland.

23. **The escape becomes a camp legend, but families pay**
    The Museum says the escape did not produce major camp-wide reprisals for reasons
    that remain unknown, but Jaster's parents were imprisoned at Auschwitz and both
    died. It also states that the escape's ingenuity raised prisoners' morale and
    quickly became a story repeated inside the camp.

## Evidence confidence

### High confidence — suitable for unqualified narration

- Names, prisoner numbers, arrival dates and the four-man escape date.
- Employment in/around HWL.
- Rubbish-detail cover.
- Prepared hatch, bump keys, forced weapons-room door.
- Stolen SS uniforms, weapons and Steyr 220.
- Bendera driving; Piechowski in senior-looking uniform and giving the gate command.
- Successful passage through the outer checkpoint.
- Car later abandoned or disabled; escape continued on foot.
- Broad post-escape fates documented by the Auschwitz Memorial.

### Attributed survivor recollection — usable with attribution

- Bendera learning that he was marked to be killed.
- The agreement to shoot themselves rather than be captured.
- Exact meter-by-meter approach to the final barrier.
- Exact wording of German abuse at the guard.
- Lempart becoming ill and being left with a priest.

### Unresolved or conflicting — do not state as settled

- Exact place and cause of the car's final stop.
- Exact German sentence shouted at the guard; published translations differ.
- A definitive account of Jaster's 1943 death.
- Popular claim that the Steyr belonged to Rudolf Höss or the deputy commandant.
- Popular claim that this escape caused Auschwitz's prisoner-tattoo policy. Do not use
  without stronger specialist confirmation.

## Story-safe dialogue policy

Verbatim or close reconstruction is permitted only for lines preserved in testimony,
especially:

- “Kazek, do something.”
- Piechowski's order to open the gate, presented as a translated reconstruction rather
  than pretending one English version is exact.

Other dialogue between the four men should be sparse and functional. It may clarify a
documented action (“The offices will be empty after midday”) but must not invent
confessions, interpersonal conflict or attitudes and present them as historical fact.

## Visual and sound proof objects

- Prisoner registration photographs and numbers.
- The rubbish trolley and kitchen ash.
- Three deliberately damaged locks and Bendera's improvised keys.
- Coal hatch, dark basement, stair route and crowbar.
- Rank insignia being sewn onto an SS uniform.
- Steyr 220 in the garage; Bendera's prisoner blouse over SS underclothes.
- Main camp clock at 3:45 p.m.
- Guard towers roughly 200 meters apart across the large guard chain.
- Barrier handle, guard booth, Piechowski's hand moving toward the holster.
- SS escape telegram and daily count register after the alarm.
- Jaster's concealed report as the post-escape payoff.

The Auschwitz Memorial's website states restrictions on cropping, graphic alteration
and commercial use of its images. Treat Museum images as research references until
usage permission is confirmed; do not assume they are production-cleared assets.
