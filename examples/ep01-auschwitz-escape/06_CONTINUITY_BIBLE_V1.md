### Character identification shorthand

- **Bendera:** broad mechanic + driver.
- **Piechowski:** compact senior uniform + holster.
- **Jaster:** tallest + hidden paper.

### Archive-reference policy

The Auschwitz Museum escape lesson contains registration or postwar portraits and may
be used internally to lock likeness. Museum terms prohibit cropping or graphic
alteration and require permission for commercial use. Do not place those images in the
final episode or feed them into a generative model until rights are cleared. Build a
separate, source-cited likeness sheet for internal review.

## Host locks

Final host identity, face, name, and channel wardrobe remain **PENDING BRAND LOCK**.
Until then, production can storyboard the host as a neutral silhouette or use a clearly
labeled stand-in.

Behavioral locks that do not depend on final casting:

- Modern field correspondent, not an escapee, guard, or omniscient character inside
  private moments.
- Direct eye contact in modern mission briefing; restrained and precise, not excited
  true-crime delivery.
- May occupy the edge of a reconstructed location or demonstrate geography/objects.
- Does not speak to the prisoners, hand them tools, open doors, point out guards, drive
  the car, or make their decisions.
- Never wears prisoner stripes or an SS uniform for entertainment.
- During the weapons-room, garage walk, car approach, and final gate, host presence
  collapses so the historical actors own the action.
- One consistent modern wardrobe across briefing and field-correspondent inserts:
  charcoal or deep navy overshirt/jacket, matte fabric, no visible brand, no military
  insignia, no costume distressing.

## Location and geography locks

### G1 — Modern mission-brief space

- Clean, dark neutral room or virtual set with one animated three-point diagram.
- Diagram always reads left to right:
  `INNER PRISONER COMPOUND → HWL / GARAGE → OUTER CHECKPOINT`.
- Do not use a generic Europe map or Holocaust montage during the first briefing.
- Object highlights: rubbish tally, improvised key, SS disguise, Steyr, barrier.
- On first mention of Auschwitz, display the restrained institutional identifier:
  `GERMAN NAZI CONCENTRATION AND EXTERMINATION CAMP`. Do not make it a dramatic title
  card or obscure it behind brand graphics.

### G2 — Auschwitz I inner prisoner compound

- Dense brick blocks, ordered paths, prisoner gate, guard oversight.
- The inner gate is architectural and embedded in the camp compound.
- The rubbish trolley passes this gate on foot.
- Never reuse the outer-checkpoint booth or barrier in this location.

### G3 — Wider guarded support zone

- Open industrial ground between the inner compound and HWL/garage.
- The camera must reveal distance and exposure: towers/guard positions, service paths,
  warehouse and garage, then the outer checkpoint farther away.
- This space explains why passing the first gate is not freedom.

### G4 — HWL / SS equipment repository

- Closed industrial repository with railway/service ramp, basement coal or coke hatch,
  dim internal corridors, wooden inner door, secured uniform/weapons room.
- Empty rail wagons may screen the hatch if used consistently.
- Interior route should remain spatially legible: hatch down → basement → internal
  doors/stairs → uniform and weapons room → side exit toward garage.
- The forced door remains visibly damaged in all later inserts.

### G5 — SS garage

- Separate low industrial building within exposed walking distance of HWL.
- Bendera crosses open ground to reach it; no covered tunnel or convenient adjacent
  door.
- Garage door, lock, car bay, and exit direction remain fixed.

### G6 — Outer checkpoint / large guard chain

- Road checkpoint at the edge of the wider guarded zone.
- Distinct from the inner gate: small guard booth, horizontal hand-operated barrier,
  long straight approach, open road beyond.
- The main guard and barrier handle must remain on the same side of the road across all
  shots.
- A secondary guard position may exist in deep background, but only one guard controls
  the barrier and receives Piechowski's order.
- Screen direction lock: the Steyr approaches left-to-right and exits left-to-right.
  Do not flip coverage during the climax.

## Hero props and vehicle

### P1 — Steyr 220

- Black Austrian Steyr 220 cabriolet/staff car; 1930s rounded body, long bonnet,
  separate fenders, period headlamps.
- Escape telegram records registration **SS-20 898**. Keep plate stable when visible.
- Left-hand drive unless vehicle-reference verification proves otherwise.
- Bendera drives; Piechowski front passenger; Jaster and Lempart in rear.
- Start condition: clean but used staff vehicle, not luxury-showroom polished.
- Do not add bullet holes, damage, chase debris, or a dramatic mechanical explosion.
- Final stop is mechanically ambiguous: engine loses usefulness or vehicle is
  abandoned; the episode does not show a definitive failed component.

### P2 — Rubbish trolley

- Four-person work trolley/cart with ash and refuse; functional, heavy, uneven wheel.
- Same wheel supplies recurring audio motif.
- It is abandoned before the men enter the warehouse route.

### P3 — Improvised keys

- Small, workmanlike metal keys—not modern lock picks and not ornate skeleton keys.
- Establish one identifiable key ring; preserve count/order across inserts.

### P4 — Crowbar

- Plain period iron crowbar sourced from basement/warehouse work tools.
- After forcing the door, wood splintering and pry damage must persist.

### P5 — Jaster's report

- Small folded document packet capable of remaining concealed beneath uniform.
- No modern envelope, printed title page, wax seal, or readable expositional headline.
- Glimpsed at 1:29–2:10, checked at 5:29–6:20 and 7:12–7:57, revealed only after the
  physical escape.

### P6 — Guard ledger and escape telegram

- Inner gate ledger is handwritten and physically local.
- Escape telegram is a separate later proof object with four identities and Steyr
  description; do not visually conflate them.

## Date, weather, and lighting

- Date: Saturday, 20 June 1942.
- Season: early summer in southern occupied Poland.
- Escape begins after lunch; vehicle departure approximately 3:45 p.m.
- Use warm but restrained late-afternoon daylight with soft-to-moderate cloud cover.
- No rain, snow, golden-hour sunset, night driving, or dramatic storm unless new
  evidence supports it.
- Interior HWL: cool, dim available light; exterior exposure remains readable.
- Final gate occurs in full afternoon light. Tension comes from visibility, not
  darkness.

## Visual grammar

- Aspect ratio: 16:9 master.
- Reconstruction look: realistic, restrained, materially specific; no sepia filter,
  fake film scratches, hyper-stylized teal/orange grade, or heroic slow motion.
- Hook: fragments under three seconds, moving from machine → faces → guard → decision.
- Briefing: stable medium host framing plus animated geography; do not turn into a
  slideshow.
- Preparation: object-led match cuts—lock, key, hatch, insignia, ignition.
- Exposure beats: longer lenses and longer holds for Bendera's garage walk and the
  final approach.
- Climax: preserve screen direction, reduce cuts as the car stops, and allow the full
  barrier crossing to complete in one readable shot.
- Violence/atrocity context: never use unrelated victim imagery as emotional wallpaper.
- Every realistic reconstruction receives a consistent small disclosure treatment,
  while archive images receive source labels.

## Audio continuity

- Recurring motifs: uneven trolley wheel; metal key clicks; Steyr engine idle; barrier
  mechanism.
- Music rises by documented pressure, not constant trailer intensity.
- Near-silence locks: failed weapons-room key, Bendera's exposed garage walk, stopped
  car.
- Strongest cue begins only after the barrier is physically cleared, then mutates into
  pursuit pulse at roll call rather than resolving triumphantly.
- No generic sirens during 1942 camp action without source justification.

## Known generation risks

1. Face drift across four shaved, similarly dressed Polish men.
2. Height drift, especially losing Jaster's documented 193 cm silhouette.
3. Swapping driver/front passenger or rear-seat positions inside the car.
4. Mirrored steering wheel, road direction, guard booth, holster, scars, or insignia.
5. Modern car geometry masquerading as a Steyr 220.
6. Generic prison fences replacing the documented two-layer geography.
7. SS uniform inaccuracies or accidental glamour treatment.
8. Modern hardware on locks, crowbar, garage, ledger, or barrier.
9. Invented chase action after the checkpoint.
10. Host interacting with or directing the historical protagonists.
11. Fabricating a readable death list or execution order to illustrate Bendera's
    attributed warning.
