# Channel Format v0

## Core promise

Do not explain history from the outside. Let the viewer experience one concrete day,
problem or choice from inside the period through a recurring host.

The format may travel to the past or the future. The stable identity is not “history
education”; it is “first-person field reporting from another time.”

## Proposed opening

### 0:00–0:08 — Consequence hook

Open on a later moment that is instantly legible without context: the host is denied
entry, caught breaking a rule, losing oxygen, hearing an evacuation order, realizing
someone will die, or facing a choice with a visible consequence.

The hook should contain:

- a recognizable world;
- a specific problem;
- the host's emotional reaction;
- one unanswered question.

### 0:08–0:14 — Brand ident

A short repeatable ident. It should not be a generic logo card, but it also must not
turn the act of time travel into a separate story beat.

### 0:14–0:45 — Present-day mission briefing

Use a direct-to-camera, podcast/YouTuber-style setup. This is not a lecture. Give only:

1. where and when we are going;
2. what the host is trying to do;
3. what can go wrong;
4. the one historical fact needed to understand the stakes.

Keep relevant visual evidence moving under or around the host. Avoid a static talking
head for the entire block.

End the briefing with a spoken launch line such as, “So let’s go back to…” or “Let’s
see what happens when…”. Cut directly on that sentence to the first historical scene.

There is **no standalone time-travel sequence**: no portal, countdown, device setup or
travel montage. The first destination shot must immediately begin the mission and pay
off the thumbnail/title promise.

## Main episode grammar

1. Arrival shock: smell, temperature, noise, crowd, danger or social rule.
2. Mission stated in-world.
3. First low-risk test exposes how the world works.
4. A local person or institution creates friction.
5. The host makes a choice that increases risk.
6. A famous event/person is approached indirectly through lived detail first.
7. The episode's central encounter or consequence pays off.
8. The host returns or debriefs with one changed belief, not a generic moral.

## Participant premise

When the episode uses participant-mode time travel, treat the host as an original
member of the dramatized scenario from the start. Build identity, clothing, transport
and blocking for that ensemble size without creating a separate “extra time traveler”
logistics subplot unless it is the episode's chosen source of conflict. Keep the
historical record and host fiction clear through framing and disclosure, not repeated
in-story apologies.

## Non-negotiable production rules

- The host is a participant, not a floating narrator.
- Context must be converted into goals, rules, objects, prices, bodily sensations and
  human interactions.
- Every 8–12 seconds, add a meaningful change in information, goal, obstacle, choice,
  interaction or proof. Every 30–45 seconds, materially change location, task,
  relationship, risk or knowledge.
- Historical/future exposition should answer a question created by the scene.
- The period should feel populated. Empty AI scenery is not enough.
- Character identity, wardrobe, props and geography require continuity QA.
- Titles and thumbnails promise a recognizable era plus an emotional problem; the
  video must deliver that problem early.
